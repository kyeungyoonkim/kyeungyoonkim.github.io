Beck's Hybrids: Seed Quality & Germination

2023–2024 · Purdue — Corporate Partners Symposium poster with Beck's Hybrids.

Context

Beck's Hybrids is the largest family-owned retail seed company in the United States and the third-largest seed brand nationally, supplying hybrid seed to farmers across the Midwest. Our student team partnered with Beck's through The Data Mine's corporate program, using field records and germination lab results to study drivers of seed quality and test performance.

Role & team

I worked alongside Austin Edwards, Willow Morningstar, Jennifer Sheng, Tri Vo, Audrey Ward, and Bruce Ward. Beck's collaborators included Will Hirschfeld, Sean Hostetler, Christian Rockwell, and Emily Tuttle; Data Mine mentors were Kevin Amstutz, Cai Chen, Doug Crabill, and Emily Hoeing, with TA support from Mihika Deshmukh.

Approach

We used R to clean the data and build analysis-ready subsets, and Python for further manipulation, statistical comparisons, and visualization. Work spanned germination outcomes, herbicide applications (including defoliant use), planting and harvest timing, and harvested moisture.

In the fall we focused on planting/harvest dates and moisture; in the spring we organized into hybrid placement and defoliant–vigor sub-teams. The goal was to highlight factors in Beck's germination pipeline that are observable in the data and, where possible, actionable.

Selected findings

  • Defoliant (defol): Compared to seed from runs without defol, saturated germination differed strongly when defol was used (Student's t-test, p ≈ 2.2×10−16); the team also tied defol use to reduced seedling vigor in complementary analyses.
  • Moisture vs. germination: Lower seed moisture aligned with better warm germination scores; cold germination versus absolute deviation from recommended moisture was explored with a trendline—no clear linear pattern emerged.
  • Timing and yield (2021 crop data): Peak planting yields appeared in late May (main window late April–May); harvest concentrated around October with an upward trend from late September through November.
  • Hybrids across locations: For the top-yielding hybrids over three years, geographic coverage varied (e.g., some locations grew nearly all top entries while others grew very few), which informs how location interacts with hybrid performance.

Takeaway

The poster argues that commercial seed operations generate rich, decision-relevant signals: moisture management, chemical programs, and calendar timing all show up in lab and field outcomes, even though weather remains a major external constraint. Natural extensions include deeper modeling of weather effects on germination.
