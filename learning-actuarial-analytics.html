Pivoting My Portfolio Toward Actuarial Analytics

#ActuarialAnalytics · #RiskModeling · #SAS

Why this direction fits

My strongest work has consistently involved applied statistics: defining measurable outcomes, cleaning imperfect data, validating assumptions, building predictive models, and communicating results to people who need to make decisions. Actuarial analytics is a natural next direction because it uses those same habits for insurance, risk, pricing, reserving, forecasting, and experience monitoring.

Skills I am emphasizing

  • Risk segmentation: defining cohorts, comparing outcomes across groups, and documenting why a grouping is meaningful.
  • Assumption discipline: writing down data definitions, exclusions, transformations, and caveats before presenting results.
  • Model validation: using baselines, distribution checks, residual thinking, and threshold analysis to avoid overtrusting a model.
  • Actuarial-ready tools: strengthening SAS, Python, R, SQL, Excel, and dashboarding workflows for repeatable reporting.
  • Communication: turning technical outputs into concise findings that explain the business implication and uncertainty.

How my projects map to actuarial work

The SAS Titanic project demonstrates reproducible data preparation, cohort summaries, formats, and documented output. The Beck's Hybrids project shows driver analysis across operational and lab datasets. My dashboards show how I communicate trends and segment-level patterns, while my professional work reinforces daily reporting, performance monitoring, and cross-functional explanation of statistical findings.

Next portfolio priorities

I plan to keep adding work that looks more directly actuarial: claim or loss trend analysis, pricing-support examples, survival or event modeling, experience study style summaries, and Excel/SAS deliverables that are easy to audit.
