Motion Detection & Localization

November 2024 · Laboratory 5 — Team embedded-vision project from hardware bring-up to a simple motion-localization rule on live camera frames.

Summary

In a group of three, we built a microcontroller imaging pipeline on a breadboard, streamed grayscale frames into Python over USB serial, and applied classical computer vision—background subtraction plus a grid-based decision rule—to highlight where motion occurs in near real time.

Team

Yoon Kim · Esha Garg · Yun-Jen Wu

Hardware setup

We wired a Raspberry Pi Pico to an Arducam HM01B0 on a breadboard (power, I²C, and parallel-style camera signals per the lab pinout: e.g. SCL/SDA, VSYNC, HREF, PCLK, data line, GND). After verifying connections, we flashed the provided camera firmware (arducam.uf2) via BOOTSEL so the Pico could capture images in the embedded environment (Thonny / MicroPython ecosystem as specified in the course materials).

Data acquisition

On the host PC we used Python’s pyserial library to open a serial link to the Pico, synchronize on a frame delimiter, and read a fixed 96×96 byte buffer into a NumPy grayscale image. Frames were displayed and buffered with Matplotlib for debugging, then fed into the vision pipeline.

Background subtraction

We used OpenCV’s cv2.createBackgroundSubtractorMOG2 (shadow detection disabled) to adapt a background model and produce a binary foreground mask: static scene content trends toward background (0), while moving regions appear as foreground (255), which is well suited when the camera and lighting are relatively stable.

Motion localization

We partitioned the frame into four quadrants. For each quadrant we counted mask pixels with grayscale intensity above 128. If that count exceeded 25% of the quadrant’s pixel count, we treated the quadrant as containing motion and drew a bounding rectangle with cv2.rectangle on the live display—turning the mask statistics into a simple, interpretable “motion here” visualization.

Challenges & notes

Serial capture is sensitive to disconnects and port contention; the lab notebook also explored robust Pico detection by USB vendor ID and handling dropped reads when the device is busy or unplugged. The approach trades model complexity for transparency: easy to tune (threshold, grid size) and to explain as a classroom motion alarm.
